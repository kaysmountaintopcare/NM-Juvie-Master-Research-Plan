## Purpose

This module provides a detailed analysis of the statutory framework governing juvenile justice in New Mexico, identifying the gap between legislative intent and operational reality.

---

## 1. The Children's Code — NMSA Chapter 32A

New Mexico's Children's Code (NMSA 1978, Chapter 32A) is the primary statutory authority governing all proceedings involving children, from delinquency adjudication through abuse/neglect intervention to mental health services.

### 1.1 Foundational Principles (Article 1)

Section 32A-1-3 articulates the Code's animating purpose:

- Children's needs are the **paramount concern**
- The family is the **preferred environment** for care and development
- Removal from the family home is a **last resort**
- When removal is necessary, the state must provide a **safe, stable, nurturing** alternative
- Children have a right to **timely permanency** in a family setting

These principles establish a clear legislative intent: the system exists to protect and rehabilitate, not to punish, and family integrity is the baseline the system is supposed to preserve.

### 1.2 Delinquency Provisions (Article 2)

**Jurisdiction (§ 32A-2-3):**
Children's court has exclusive jurisdiction over delinquent acts committed by persons under age 18 (with youthful offender provisions for ages 14–17 for certain serious offenses transferable to adult court under § 32A-2-20).

**Intake and Diversion (§ 32A-2-7 through § 32A-2-9):**
CYFD probation officers conduct intake screenings and may divert cases from formal processing. The Code permits diversion but does not require outcome tracking for diverted youth — a critical gap.

**Detention (§ 32A-2-11 through § 32A-2-13):**
Pre-adjudication detention is permitted only when the child poses a risk of flight, danger, or is already under supervision. Detention hearings must occur within 24 hours. The Code requires a probable-cause determination but does not explicitly mandate that risk-assessment instruments (like SDM) be followed without override.

**Predisposition Study (§ 32A-2-14):**
Before disposition, the court must receive a report evaluating:
- The child's physical, mental, and social condition
- The family environment
- Available community resources
- The child's educational status and needs

This provision implicitly requires family involvement, but does not mandate that parents receive the report or participate in its preparation.

**Dispositional Alternatives (§ 32A-2-17):**
The court may order:
- Probation with or without conditions
- Community service
- Restitution
- Placement in a community-based facility
- Commitment to CYFD custody

Commitment to CYFD is the most restrictive disposition and triggers CYFD's authority over placement, programming, and release decisions.

**Commitment to CYFD (§ 32A-2-19):**
- Maximum duration is set by statute based on offense severity
- CYFD determines facility placement, transfers, and programming
- Periodic judicial review is required but frequency is not tightly specified
- The statute does not grant parents a right to petition for placement review between scheduled hearings

### 1.3 Families in Need of Court-Ordered Services (Article 3B)

Article 3B allows courts to order services when a child's behavior endangers their welfare without requiring a delinquency finding. This is a less restrictive alternative to delinquency adjudication, but:
- Service availability varies dramatically by county
- There is no statutory mandate for CYFD to develop services in underserved areas
- Parents may not be aware this pathway exists

### 1.4 Abuse and Neglect (Article 4)

Article 4 governs child protection proceedings. Its relevance to juvenile justice includes:
- **Dual-system youth:** Children who are simultaneously delinquency-involved and abuse/neglect victims experience compounding system contact with distinct legal standards and different attorneys
- **Parental rights:** Article 4 provides stronger parental protections (right to counsel, explicit notice requirements) than Article 2's delinquency provisions — creating a paradox where parents have more rights when accused of harming their child than when the state confines their child

### 1.5 Mental Health (Article 6A)

Article 6A mandates access to mental health evaluation and treatment for children. Chronic under-resourcing of this article is a direct contributor to the behavioral health service gap documented by the 2025 LFC report.

### 1.6 Juvenile Public Safety Advisory Board (Article 7A)

Replaced the former Juvenile Parole Board. The JPSAB:
- Advises CYFD on juvenile justice policy
- Reviews individual cases for release decisions
- Has **no independent investigative power**
- Has **no binding authority** — recommendations can be ignored

### 1.7 Family Representation and Advocacy Act (Article 27)

Enacted to improve legal representation for families in child welfare (abuse/neglect) proceedings. Coverage does not consistently extend to delinquency cases, leaving parents of JJS-involved youth without guaranteed representation.

### 1.8 Office of Child Advocate (Article 30)

Created an independent office to:
- Monitor CYFD across all divisions
- Receive and investigate complaints
- Issue public reports

The office operates with limited staff and budget and lacks subpoena power, significantly constraining its effectiveness.

## 2. Federal Framework

### 2.1 Juvenile Justice and Delinquency Prevention Act (JJDPA)

The JJDPA (reauthorized 2018) conditions federal funding on state compliance with four core requirements:
1. **Deinstitutionalization of status offenders** — youth whose conduct would not be criminal for adults may not be detained
2. **Sight and sound separation** — juveniles must be separated from adult inmates
3. **Jail removal** — juveniles may not be held in adult jails or lockups beyond narrow exceptions
4. **Racial and ethnic disparity reduction** — states must address disproportionate contact at every decision point

New Mexico receives approximately $1.2 million annually in JJDPA formula grant funding.

### 2.2 IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act)

Requires continued educational services for youth with disabilities in **all** settings, including secure facilities. Youth R.'s IEP history illustrates how this federal mandate is honored on paper but frequently violated in practice during facility transitions.

### 2.3 FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act)

Grants parents access to educational records and restricts unauthorized disclosure. Relevant to cases where parents are denied access to facility-based school records for their own child.

## 3. Identified Statutory Gaps

| Gap | Consequence |
|-----|-------------|
| No mandatory post-diversion outcome tracking | Diverted youth disappear from the data — no way to know if diversion works |
| No parental right to counsel in delinquency proceedings | Parents navigate the system without legal support while CYFD has institutional counsel |
| No enforceable minimum behavioral health staffing standards in facilities | Staffing varies by budget cycle, not by clinical need |
| No statutory mechanism for parents to petition for emergency placement review | Parents must wait for scheduled hearings while their child may be in an inappropriate setting |
| No mandatory disparity reporting with corrective-action triggers | Racial/ethnic disparities persist without systemic accountability |
| No explicit prohibition on SDM risk-assessment overrides without documented justification | Officials detain low-risk youth without recorded rationale |
| Stronger parental protections in abuse/neglect (Art. 4) than in delinquency (Art. 2) | Parents of system-involved youth have fewer rights than parents accused of harming their child |

## 4. Constitutional Dimensions

- **Fourteenth Amendment (Due Process):** Parents have a constitutionally protected liberty interest in the care, custody, and management of their children (Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000)). System practices that override parental authority without adequate notice and hearing may violate substantive and procedural due process.
- **Fourteenth Amendment (Equal Protection):** Persistent racial/ethnic disparities in detention and commitment decisions, particularly when risk-assessment overrides are not tracked by race, raise equal protection concerns.
- **First Amendment:** Retaliation patterns against parents who report system failures or seek media attention implicate protected speech and petition rights.
