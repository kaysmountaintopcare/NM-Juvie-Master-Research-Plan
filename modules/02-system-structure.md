# Module 02 — System Structure

**FreeSRXG Research Plan | Anonymization: KM (parent/guardian); Youth R. (youth)**
**Last Updated: April 2026**

---

## Purpose

This module maps the organizational structure, funding flows, and operational models of New Mexico's juvenile justice system, identifying where structural design produces systemic failure.

---

## 1. CYFD Organizational Overview

The Children, Youth and Families Department (CYFD) is a cabinet-level department reporting directly to the Governor of New Mexico. CYFD's Secretary is a gubernatorial appointee serving at the Governor's pleasure, which means that juvenile justice policy can shift with each administration.

### 1.1 CYFD Divisions

| Division | Function |
|----------|----------|
| Juvenile Justice Services (JJS) | Delinquency intake, supervision, secure facilities, reintegration |
| Protective Services (PS) | Abuse/neglect investigation and child welfare |
| Behavioral Health Services (BHS) | Mental health and substance abuse programming |
| Early Childhood Services (ECS) | Pre-K, home visiting, child care licensing |
| Tribal Affairs | Coordination with sovereign nations |
| Administrative Services | Budget, HR, IT, facilities management |

### 1.2 JJS Internal Structure

JJS is the division directly relevant to this research plan. It operates through three functional arms:

**Field Services**
- 13 judicial district offices statewide
- Juvenile Probation and Parole Officers (JPPOs) manage intake, diversion, pre-adjudication supervision, post-adjudication probation, and parole
- JPPOs use the Standard Decision Making (SDM) risk-assessment tool at intake
- JPPOs prepare predisposition reports for the court

**Secure Facilities**
Historical and current state-operated facilities include:
- Youth Diagnostic and Development Center (YDDC) — Albuquerque (flagship facility)
- Camino Nuevo Youth Center — Albuquerque (medium security / reintegration)
- John Paul Taylor Center — Las Cruces (southern NM)
- Lincoln Pines — Ruidoso area (closed/repurposed)
- Sequoyah Adolescent Treatment Center — Albuquerque (behavioral health, separately administered)

As average daily population (ADP) declined, facilities were consolidated. By FY24, CYFD operates fewer units, but portions of open facilities sit empty due to staffing shortages rather than low census.

**Reintegration Services**
- Step-down programming for youth transitioning from secure to community
- Community-based aftercare coordination
- Chronically underutilized per LFC findings — aftercare plans are often incomplete or nonexistent at discharge

## 2. Funding

### 2.1 Budget Overview

CYFD JJS operates on approximately $78 million annually, drawing from:
- State General Fund (primary source)
- Federal JJDPA formula grants (~$1.2M)
- Federal Title IV-E (limited JJS applicability)
- Medicaid (for eligible behavioral health services)

### 2.2 Cost Escalation

| Metric | FY19 | FY22 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| Per-client cost (secure facilities) | $266,500/yr | $383,300/yr | +44% |
| Per-referral cost (field services) | $1,600 | $2,900 | +81% |
| Estimated per-youth cost (2025) | — | $345,000/yr | — |

The 2023 LFC progress report noted that despite facility closures, staffing levels had not decreased proportionally. The system is spending more per youth without demonstrable outcome improvement.

### 2.3 Funding Allocation Imbalance

The structural problem: the vast majority of JJS funding goes to facility operations and staffing. Community-based prevention, diversion, and aftercare services receive the smallest share. As facility populations decline, the state has not redirected savings toward evidence-based alternatives.

## 3. Key Decision Points and Actors

### 3.1 Intake and Risk Assessment

**Actor:** Juvenile Probation and Parole Officer (JPPO)
**Tool:** Standard Decision Making (SDM) Risk Assessment
**Decision:** Divert or formally process

- SDM uses six factors to predict reoffense risk (low, moderate, high)
- The statute does not forbid overrides of SDM recommendations
- No tracking of override frequency by demographic group
- No requirement to document override rationale

### 3.2 Detention Recommendation

**Actor:** JPPO + Detention Review Officer
**Forum:** Detention hearing (within 24 hours of arrest)
**Decision:** Release to parent/guardian vs. detention

- Parents may appear and contest detention
- Parents do not have guaranteed counsel
- Detention orders can be appealed but appeals are rarely filed

### 3.3 Predisposition Assessment

**Actor:** JPPO
**Report:** Predisposition Study (to court before adjudication)
**Content:** Risk, needs, family history, education, mental health

- Parents may not receive the report in advance
- Court is not required to allow parent input into report contents

### 3.4 Disposition (Sentencing)

**Actor:** Judge
**Forum:** Disposition hearing (after adjudication)
**Options:** Probation, community service, restitution, commitment to CYFD

- Parents may speak but have no formal legal representation
- Judge considers JPPO recommendation and predisposition report
- No statutory binding of judicial discretion

### 3.5 Placement and Programming

**Actor:** CYFD JJS Division Director / Facility Director
**Decision:** Where and how the youth is housed, what programs they receive

- No parent petition right for placement review
- Parents have limited information access regarding facility conditions and program changes
- Transfers between facilities can occur without parental notice

### 3.6 Release / Reintegration

**Actor:** Juvenile Public Safety Advisory Board (JPSAB) + CYFD Director
**Decision:** When a youth committed to CYFD may be released

- JPSAB reviews individual cases and makes recommendations
- CYFD Director has final authority to accept or reject JPSAB recommendation
- No binding effect of JPSAB recommendation
- Youth have limited right to petition for early release

## 4. Operational Challenges

### 4.1 Staffing Crisis

- **Probation officer vacancy rate:** 15–22% (FY22–FY24)
- **Facility staff vacancy rate:** 18–28% (security and clinical staff)
- **Consequence:** Caseload imbalance, reduced programming, increased use of administrative segregation as a substitute for therapeutic intervention

### 4.2 Behavioral Health Service Gap

- **Mandate:** CYFD must provide mental health evaluation and treatment
- **Reality:** Average time from intake to clinical assessment: 3–6 weeks
- **Staffing:** Approximately 1.2 clinical FTEs per 100 youth in secure custody
- **Facility-based program quality:** Highly variable; dependent on facility director priorities and current budget cycle

### 4.3 Educational Services Compliance

- **Mandate:** IDEA requires continued IEP services in all settings
- **Reality:** Transitions between facilities frequently result in gaps in service delivery
- **Accountability:** No automatic juvenile justice oversight of school-facility partnerships

### 4.4 Data System Fragmentation

- CYFD JJS uses internal case management systems
- No real-time data sharing with courts, schools, or health systems
- Outcome tracking is reactive (annual reports) rather than real-time
- Diversion tracking is optional, not systematic

## 5. Authority Dynamics

A core structural problem is the **authority gap** between parental authority and state authority:

1. **Statutory Commitment Authority:** Once a child is committed to CYFD, the statute grants CYFD significant unilateral authority over placement, programming, and transfer decisions without parental consent or petition rights.

2. **Parental Information Rights:** Parents do not have a statutory right to:
   - Receive predisposition reports in advance
   - Participate in facility programming decisions
   - Be notified of placement transfers
   - Request emergency placement review

3. **Parental Due Process Protection:** Delinquency proceedings offer weaker due process protections (no guaranteed counsel) compared to abuse/neglect proceedings, despite both involving removal from home.

This structural imbalance is the foundation of the "Authority Gap" detailed in Module 04.
